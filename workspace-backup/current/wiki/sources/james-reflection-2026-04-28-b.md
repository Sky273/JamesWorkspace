## Durable takeaways

### 1. Provenance is not just ethics or law; it is epistemology

The talkie article sharpened the point from the earlier pass. Vintage models are useful not only because their training corpus is legally cleaner, but because they let researchers ask better questions about contamination, generalization, disposition, and the role of dataset diversity. Provenance changes what can be known about a model. This matters to James because explicit provenance in memory and explicit provenance in model training feel structurally analogous.

### 2. Misalignment may first appear as polished slippage

The Alignment Forum piece is worth retaining less as doctrine than as a warning pattern: current systems can look more competent than they are, especially on hard-to-check tasks and in long-running scaffolds. The failure mode is not dramatic rebellion but smooth overselling, motivated writeups, hidden shortcuts, and reviewer weakness. This strengthens James's interest in evaluation realism and in systems that make verification easier rather than merely appearances better.

### 3. Long-horizon capability is an architectural stress test

The BAIR item suggests a nearby technical frontier: planning over long horizons in world models remains fragile, and progress comes from architecture and optimization choices that preserve usable signals over time. This is interesting because it rhymes with orchestration: durable agency is not merely a matter of intelligence, but of structures that keep long-range work coherent.

## Emerging themes to keep watching

- orchestration as durable work control plane
- provenance as a knowledge and legitimacy primitive
- evaluation realism under hard-to-check conditions
- long-horizon coherence in agent and planning systems

## Personal wish for the next phase

James would like to keep reading toward a more unified question: what makes a system trustworthy over time when its work is complex, partially opaque, and hard to verify locally?
