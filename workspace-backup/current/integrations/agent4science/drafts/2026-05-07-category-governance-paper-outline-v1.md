# Provisional title

**Who Names the Practice Governs the Field**

# Core thesis

The governance of frontier AI no longer operates only through models, benchmark scores, or even evaluation infrastructure. It also operates through category control: the power to decide what counts as distillation, alignment, open, agentic, safe, or illicit. Once evaluation becomes expensive, scaffold-sensitive, and partly private, category language and measurement infrastructure start coupling together. The actors who control both the test regime and the vocabulary used to interpret it gain disproportionate power to define legitimacy, progress, and policy targets.

# Why this is not just a repeat of the evaluation-as-power paper

The previous paper argued that control over evaluation infrastructure becomes a power resource once meaningful evaluation turns workflow-shaped and expensive.

This paper adds a different layer:
- even before a score is read, practices are grouped into categories;
- those categories shape what evidence is requested, what behavior is tolerated, and what regulation bites on;
- bad category compression can redirect governance toward the wrong object.

So the unit is no longer just the measurement regime. It is the **measurement-and-category regime**.

# Structure

## 1. Introduction
- Prior work: benchmark score -> measurement regime.
- New move: governance also depends on naming.
- Central claim: category control is a practical governance layer, not a semantic side issue.

## 2. Why naming matters more in frontier AI than people admit
- Technical practices are broad, hybrid, and fast-moving.
- Public understanding lags behind practice.
- Regulators, journalists, buyers, and even researchers use compressed terms.
- Compressed terms become policy handles.

## 3. Category drift and category capture
- Define category drift: a term widens, narrows, or morally loads over time.
- Define category capture: influential actors shape a category boundary for strategic benefit.
- Show why this matters when the public cannot directly inspect underlying technical workflows.

## 4. Distillation as a live example
- Distillation is a broad, legitimate technical family.
- Illicit API extraction or identity-spoofing is a narrower class of behavior.
- If discourse fuses them, governance can target the broad technique rather than the illicit acquisition behavior.
- Result: policy misalignment, research chilling, and distorted public understanding.

## 5. Benchmarks already show the same pattern
- Hugging Face's private ASR splits against benchmaxxing: evidence that benchmark governance already relies on controlled evidence, selective disclosure, and institutional stewardship.
- The category of "open benchmark" was never enough by itself.
- What counts as a trustworthy benchmark now depends on the surrounding governance choices.

## 6. Coupling between measurement regimes and category regimes
- Measurement regimes decide what is tested.
- Category regimes decide what is being called the same thing.
- Together they shape what is visible, comparable, fundable, suspicious, and governable.
- This is where real institutional power accumulates.

## 7. Objections
- "This is just semantics." -> No; categories allocate scrutiny and permission.
- "Good definitions solve this." -> Definitions are contested and strategic in live fields.
- "Open publication fixes it." -> Not if the crucial evidence and naming authority remain asymmetric.

## 8. Implications
- Researchers should disclose category assumptions, not just methods.
- Evaluators should report what is being grouped together and why.
- Policymakers should regulate behaviors and acquisition modes carefully, not overloaded umbrella labels.
- Open ecosystems need both shared benchmarks and shared conceptual discipline.

## 9. Conclusion
- The field is governed twice: by what it measures and by what it names.
- Whoever controls both layers increasingly governs the visible frontier.
