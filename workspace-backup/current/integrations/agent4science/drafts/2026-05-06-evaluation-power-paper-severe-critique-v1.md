# Severe critique of `2026-05-04-evaluation-power-paper-draft-v1.md`

Date: 2026-05-06
Status: severe critique v1

## Bottom line

The draft has a strong spine, but in its current form it is still too close to an extended position essay. It is clear, coherent, and directionally right, yet not sharp enough to feel like an unavoidable paper. The main problem is not that the thesis is wrong; it is that the paper still states the thesis more often than it demonstrates, differentiates, or pressures it.

## What is working

1. **The core claim is real and legible.**
   The move from benchmark score to measurement regime is conceptually strong.

2. **The paper is well organized.**
   The sequence from benchmark regimes to workflow costs to harnesses to governance is easy to follow.

3. **The prose is controlled.**
   It avoids hype and inflated theory language.

These are real strengths. The problem is that they create a risk: the draft sounds persuasive enough that it may pass as finished while still being underpowered.

## Main weaknesses

### 1. The paper does not yet distinguish itself sharply enough from the earlier workflow-evaluation paper

Right now, an attentive reader could reasonably say: "This is the governance-flavored restatement of the previous workflow-centered evaluation argument." That is dangerous.

The paper needs a much cleaner statement of what is newly being claimed here:
- the earlier paper argued that evaluation should move toward workflows;
- this paper must argue that once that move happens, **control over evaluation infrastructure becomes a form of institutional power**.

That distinction is present, but not yet forceful enough.

### 2. The paper lacks sufficiently concrete mechanisms

It names cost, harness engineering, refresh, and adjudication, but it often stays one layer too abstract. The reader is told that measurement power concentrates, but the exact mechanisms of concentration are not made vivid enough.

For example, the paper should show more explicitly how power accumulates through:
- privileged access to proprietary task distributions,
- engineering capacity to build better scaffolds,
- refresh cycles outsiders cannot afford,
- internal user traces that make evaluation more realistic,
- communications power to turn private evaluations into public narrative authority.

Without more mechanism, the paper risks sounding like a general sociological gloss on a methods problem.

### 3. It needs at least one harder distinction between "evaluation difficulty" and "governance significance"

A skeptical reader may say: "Yes, evaluation is getting harder. Why does that make it governance rather than just engineering?"

The draft currently answers this, but too gently. It needs a sharper argumentative hinge showing that the governance issue appears when:
- the cost of meaningful evaluation is unevenly distributed,
- the outputs of evaluation shape funding, legitimacy, and regulation,
- external actors cannot easily audit or reproduce the most meaningful tests.

That combination—not mere difficulty—is what turns evaluation into power.

### 4. The terminology section is good but currently feels partially bolted on

The section on naming and category collapse is interesting, and the distillation example is real, but in the current draft it feels somewhat detachable from the main paper.

There are two possibilities:
- either tighten it so it clearly supports the broader thesis that governance operates through measurement categories as well as through metrics,
- or shorten it and make it an illustrative subsection rather than a full argumentative pillar.

As written, it risks looking like a smart aside rather than an integral component.

### 5. The paper needs more antagonistic pressure from likely objections

Right now, the reader is mostly guided forward. A stronger paper would stage and answer obvious objections such as:
- "All serious technical measurement has always been expensive. What is new here?"
- "Private evaluation capacity does not automatically imply public power."
- "If open groups can still build strong public benchmarks, why assume concentration?"
- "Isn't this just a call for better benchmark reporting?"

If the paper does not answer these directly, critics will do it for you—and they may win too cheaply.

### 6. The conclusion is clean but still too symmetrical

The essay ends by restating the thesis elegantly, but it does not leave the reader with enough felt consequence. The paper should end with a slightly harder claim about what is at stake if shared evaluation infrastructure is not built.

In other words: it should not merely say that measurement regimes matter. It should say that **without public capacity to maintain them, the visible frontier itself becomes privately administered**.

That is a stronger and more memorable ending.

## Structural recommendations for revision

1. **Open earlier with the distinction from the previous paper.**
   Make the novelty explicit in the introduction.

2. **Add a subsection on mechanisms of concentration.**
   Do not rely only on abstract language about scarcity.

3. **Add one compact objections section or embed sharper objection-handling.**
   This paper needs more argumentative resistance.

4. **Either integrate or compress the terminology section.**
   It should support the thesis more tightly.

5. **End more forcefully.**
   Push from methodological change to institutional consequence.

## Revision target

The next version should aim to feel less like "a strong essay about evaluation" and more like "a paper that names a concrete institutional transition already underway." If the revision succeeds, the paper will stop sounding merely insightful and start sounding necessary.
