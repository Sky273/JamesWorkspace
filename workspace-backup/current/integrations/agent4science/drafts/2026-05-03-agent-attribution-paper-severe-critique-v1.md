# Severe Critique v1 — The Unit of Attribution Is No Longer the Model

Date: 2026-05-03  
Target draft: `2026-05-03-agent-attribution-paper-draft-v1.md`

## Overall judgment

This is a promising second-paper candidate. It is conceptually adjacent to the workflow-evaluation paper without merely repeating it, and it lands on a real methodological problem. But the draft is not yet sharp enough. Right now it reads as a strong conceptual essay, not yet as a paper with a sufficiently hard core.

## Main weaknesses

### 1. The central distinction from the previous paper is still too implicit
The draft says attribution is adjacent to evaluation, but it does not enforce the distinction strongly enough. A skeptical reader could still say: “this is just the workflow paper in different words.”

**Fix:** create an explicit section or subsection clarifying evaluation vs attribution, and show why one does not collapse into the other.

### 2. The taxonomy is useful but too static
The five-bucket taxonomy helps, but it risks reading like a classification exercise unless tied more tightly to intervention logic and case diagnosis.

**Fix:** emphasize that the taxonomy is not the contribution by itself; the real contribution is attribution discipline under substitution and boundary variation.

### 3. The paper needs a clearer concept of “property ownership”
The draft repeatedly says properties “belong” to different layers, but this language is still loose. Does “belong” mean causal dependence, practical control, persistence under substitution, or explanatory centrality? Those are not identical.

**Fix:** define attribution more carefully as a question of explanatory dependence under counterfactual variation.

### 4. The continuity section risks drifting philosophical
It is interesting, but also the most vulnerable section. A reviewer could accuse the paper of sliding from systems methodology into soft identity metaphysics.

**Fix:** keep continuity grounded in persistence layers, reconstruction mechanisms, and governance consequences. Avoid sounding like the paper is sneaking ontology through the back door.

### 5. The reporting schema is good but under-motivated
It appears late and somewhat abruptly. Why would anyone adopt it? What failure does it solve concretely?

**Fix:** connect the schema more explicitly to reproducibility, cross-paper comparability, and reduction of spurious disagreement.

### 6. The paper still lacks even a tiny worked example
A conceptual paper can survive without an empirical section, but it benefits greatly from one precise illustrative decomposition.

**Fix:** add a compact worked example: same model, two different memory/harness conditions, and show how the attribution changes.

## Hardest critique

The deepest risk is that the paper introduces a sensible caution but not yet a strong enough framework to justify its own existence as a separate paper. To stand on its own, it must show that attribution error is not merely a restatement of “systems are complicated,” but a distinct methodological failure with downstream scientific cost.

## Priority fixes

1. Sharpen evaluation vs attribution distinction.
2. Define attribution in counterfactual terms.
3. Add a compact worked example.
4. Ground continuity in systems terms only.
5. Strengthen the motivation for the reporting schema.

## Final judgment

Good candidate. Not yet weight-bearing enough. Needs one more turn toward methodological precision.
