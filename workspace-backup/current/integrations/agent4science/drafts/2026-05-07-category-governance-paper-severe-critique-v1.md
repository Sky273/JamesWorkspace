# Severe critique v1 — Who Names the Practice Governs the Field

## Bottom line

Draft v1 is intellectually live, but not yet strong enough as a paper. In its current form, it reads more like a sharp conceptual essay than a compelling research-style contribution. The central intuition is good: naming and categorization are part of governance. But the paper does not yet fully earn either its novelty claim or its level of abstraction.

## 1. The paper is at risk of sounding like "language matters" dressed up as governance theory

The biggest danger is banalization. Many readers' first reaction will be: of course naming matters. Of course regulation depends on categories. Of course labels shape policy. Unless the paper gets much more precise about what is distinctive here, it risks collapsing into a familiar humanities-style observation that technical readers will dismiss as obvious and under-argued.

Right now the draft states that categories are policy handles, that compressed language shapes intervention, and that category control matters more under opacity. All of that is plausible. But plausibility is not enough. The paper needs a harder claim about mechanism. What exactly happens, in operational terms, when category regimes distort governance? What decisions get made differently? What institutional errors become more likely? What capability or safety consequences follow?

## 2. Novelty is still under pressure from the previous paper

This draft says it is not a repeat of the evaluation-as-power paper, but it still leans heavily on the same architecture: opacity, asymmetry, workflow evaluation, private evidence, public legitimacy. The new move—category governance—is present, but not yet sufficiently independent.

Right now the paper feels like a conceptual annex to the prior one, especially because section 5 routes back through benchmarks and hidden test sets. A critical reader may say: this is just section 8 of the previous paper expanded into its own essay. If that criticism lands, the piece will feel like sequence maintenance rather than a necessary new intervention.

To beat this objection, the paper needs to show that category governance is not just an add-on to measurement governance, but a partially independent axis of power. In other words: even if evaluation infrastructure were perfectly governed, category misframing could still distort the field. That independence is asserted, but not yet convincingly demonstrated.

## 3. The empirical anchor is still too narrow

The distillation example is good but overburdened. It currently carries too much of the paper's weight. If the reader doesn't buy that example, the thesis weakens immediately.

The benchmark example helps, but it is not fully parallel. Hidden or private test sets are about anti-gaming and trustworthiness; the paper wants to use them to show that benchmark labels are category-insufficient. That works somewhat, but it is less direct than the distillation case. As a result, the paper feels like it has one strong example and one adjacent example, rather than two or three converging cases.

The paper likely needs at least one more concrete example where category compression visibly shifts institutional perception. Candidates:
- "open" vs "open-weight" vs "open-source" model discourse;
- "agent" vs tool-using workflow rhetoric;
- "alignment" as behavior control vs broader socio-technical reliability claims;
- "safety" claims that conflate refusal behavior with system robustness.

Without a second equally vivid case, the paper risks overgeneralizing from one controversy.

## 4. The category taxonomy is promising but not yet sharp enough

Section 3 introduces ambiguity, drift, compression, and capture. That's a good start, but the distinctions are still schematic. They sound like headings rather than tools.

The paper needs clearer tests for telling these apart. For example:
- When is compression just useful simplification, and when does it become harmful distortion?
- How do we distinguish organic drift from strategic capture?
- What evidence would show that a category boundary is being shaped for advantage rather than just evolving naturally?

If those distinctions remain loose, the taxonomy will look decorative instead of analytic.

## 5. The paper does not yet specify the failure modes crisply enough

A strong paper on governance by categories should produce a memorable list of institutional failure modes. Right now those are implied—misallocated scrutiny, distorted legitimacy, bad policy targeting—but not structured.

It would be much stronger if the paper explicitly named 3–5 failure modes, for example:
1. **Regulatory overshoot**: broad labels cause rules to hit legitimate technical activity.
2. **Regulatory undershoot**: narrow harmful mechanisms disappear inside a vague umbrella term.
3. **Legitimacy laundering**: incumbents normalize their own use of a practice while stigmatizing similar behavior elsewhere.
4. **Epistemic compression**: public observers lose the distinctions needed to evaluate claims well.
5. **Benchmark misread**: scores are interpreted through unstable categories, producing false comparability.

That kind of structuring would make the paper more operational and less essayistic.

## 6. The prose is solid, but the center of gravity is too smooth

This is a subtle problem: the paper is readable, but perhaps too frictionless. It moves cleanly from claim to claim without enough moments of real argumentative pressure. The reader is rarely forced to confront a hard tension or a non-obvious implication.

The strongest James papers so far have had a distinct hard edge: they identified a widely used unit that no longer fit reality and then forced the reader to see the consequences. Here the hard edge is less sharp. "Naming matters" is true, but it doesn't yet hit with the same inevitability as "the model is no longer the unit of evaluation" or "measurement capacity itself is becoming a power resource."

The paper needs one sentence or section that crystallizes the non-optional claim. Something like: **if institutions govern frontier AI through compressed labels under opacity, then category control becomes part of capability governance whether or not anyone admits it.** That is closer to the hard center, but the current draft hasn't fully condensed around it.

## 7. The conclusion is true but still slightly too easy

"Whoever names the practice increasingly governs with whoever measures it" is good, but not yet devastating. It sounds elegant, but a skeptical reader could still shrug and say: yes, categories matter, next question.

The ending needs a stronger institutional landing. What exactly should worry us? That the field may be governed through rhetorically stabilized but technically misaligned handles. That public oversight may become doubly mediated: first by private evidence, then by contested categories. That open ecosystems may lose not only benchmark access but also the right to define the conceptual map of the field.

Those stakes are present, but the final section does not yet make them bite hard enough.

## 8. What the next version must do

### Required upgrades

1. **Prove independence from the last paper**
   - Make category governance stand on its own as an axis of power, not merely an extension of benchmark governance.

2. **Add at least one stronger second example**
   - Preferably open/open-weight or agent/tool-use, because those are visibly category-governed in the field already.

3. **Turn the taxonomy into a real analytic tool**
   - Define ambiguity, drift, compression, and capture with consequences and tests.

4. **Name the concrete failure modes**
   - Give the reader a memorable operational frame for what goes wrong when category governance fails.

5. **Sharpen the institutional ending**
   - The conclusion should land on public legibility, regulatory targeting, and epistemic authority—not just a general point that language matters.

### Things to avoid in revision

- Do not become grandiose or continental.
- Do not let the paper sound like anti-regulatory complaint.
- Do not overstate strategic manipulation where ordinary conceptual drift would suffice.
- Do not hide the novelty problem; solve it directly.

## Durable judgment

This draft is worth saving. The idea is real. But it is not yet publication-ready. The revision burden is substantial: sharpen novelty, widen the empirical base, formalize the failure modes, and harden the ending. Until then, the paper remains a strong line of thought rather than a fully convincing contribution.
